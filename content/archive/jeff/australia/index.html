We've Come From a Land Down Under

G'day mates! Our journey to Australia began with an 18-hour flight to Melbourne, which I was dreading because I'd never been on a plane more than 5 hours, and that was a miserable experience! Thankfully, my dad had the foresight to book us in Business Class. Let me tell you, if you ever fly international, GO BUSINESS CLASS!! It is more than worth the expense. Our seats were on the secluded upper deck of the massive 747, complete with lazy boy-style reclining chairs, individual TV monitors, headphones, slippers and slumbermasks. Shortly after takeoff we were treated to a surprisingly delicious dinner of smoked salmon w/capers, garden salad, shrimp stir-fry w/snap peas, caramel brownie fudge tart, wine and coffee. We then reclined our chairs and slept peacefully most of the night until we were awakened by the smells of hot coffee, spinach and mushroom omelets, and fresh fruit. This is the way to travel!

We spent our first three days in Melbourne, culture and restaurant capital of Australia. We took a driving tour through the Dandenong Mountains, which are thick with mountain ash, the second tallest trees in the world next to the California redwoods. A brief wallaby (small kangaroo) sighting proved to be one of the few we had the entire trip, aside from several lizards and exotic birds. We drove on through the Yarra Valley, where some of Australia's best wines are produced, and toured three local vineyards tucked away in the rolling green hills of the Valley. We enjoyed several tastings of some fabulous vintages and had lunch at Fergusson's winery, where I seized my first opportunity to try emu, a local delicacy. Emu is a large bird similar to an ostrich that tastes very much like beef. While in Melbourne, I also sampled another exotic dish, seared kangaroo. Again, very tasty similar to beef. For some reason, I had the impression that kangaroos were a protected species that the Aussies wouldn't dare eat, but according to the locals, they have reached near plague-like numbers and are very destructive to crops and topsoil. You do need a special permit to hunt them, but it is relatively easy to obtain and allows you to take out as many as 300 of the little buggers at a time! Our last night in Melbourne we toured the city aboard the historic Tram Car Restaurant, a 1920's-era railcar that has been refurbished and converted into a rolling restaurant. It's quite an elegant production, if a bit impractical. The ride was, as you can imagine, not a smooth one and some of the areas we traveled through were suspect. However, the food was delicious, and aside from being mistaken for a gay couple, it was an enjoyable evening!

Our next stop was Ayers Rock, located in the Red Center of Australia, otherwise known as the Outback. It was hot as Hades in Ayers Rock, similar to Arizona in the summer, and the intense sun made it seem even hotter. The two geological icons of this region are Ayers Rock and The Olgas, or Uluru (oo-loo-ROO) and Kata Tjuta (KAH-tah JOO-tah) to the Aborigines, the native people of the land. Before arriving, we had intended to climb Uluru, a 1000 ft. red sandstone monolith that rises out of the infinitely flat desert floor. This rock is quite amazing, creating its own weather patterns and mini ecosystems around its base. The Aborigines have long revered this place as the holiest of holy, believing all life comes from Uluru, and they worship it accordingly. After hearing our guide speak about its religious significance and after visiting the Aboriginal cultural center where this view was reinforced, we realized it was a sacred place and climbing it would be blasphemy. I'm not a religious man, but I do consider myself spiritual, and out of respect, we did NOT climb it. Instead, we bought patches for our backpacks from the cultural center that say "I Did NOT Climb Uluru!" We completed our experience in the Red Center at the "Sounds of Silence" dinner held on a large mesa overlooking the rocks. The night began with a champagne toast at sunset accompanied by a digeridoo player, followed by an elaborate four-course meal by candlelight. We enjoyed crocodile (a bit like chicken, only tougher) salad, emu sausages, lamb shanks, kangaroo loin and a multitude of deserts, including macadamia nut pie. Yummy! It was our best meal by far, except for the multitude of insects trying to share our meals. After dinner, the candles were blown out and an astronomer took us on a fascinating tour of the crystal clear sky, bright with the light of a billion stars.

Our next stop was a visit to Cairns (pronounced CANS), a tropical sweatbox on the Northeast coast surrounded by lush rainforest, where the humidity was a stifling 95%. It was quite an adjustment just having come from the driest place on the continent. I dragged my dad on a hike up the 1000 ft. Mt. Whitfield because of the promise of great views of the beaches and city. The steep trail was overgrown in places with sharp grasses and impenetrable brush, and the humidity was suffocating. This having been our first strenuous exercise since we arrived, our pace to the top was less than brisk. People we met along the trail proclaimed, "It's worth it, mates", sensing we needed a little motivation after seeing our sweat-drenched scowls. However, when we arrived at the top, the view was obscured by soaring trees and brush, although we did have a nice view of the airport (yippee!). Oh well, what's that saying…It's all about the journey, not the destination? Right…

The highlight of the trip for me was an excursion to Agincourt Reef, one of the many smaller coral reefs that make up the Great Barrier Reef. My dad lounged on the sun deck and I chose to snorkel. My first time in the water was less than enjoyable as I was stung about a dozen times by box jellyfish forcing me from the water after only 15 minutes. Feeling soundly defeated, I overheard someone say "stinger suits" were available, which is something like a wet suit, only much lighter weight, that protects swimmers from jellyfish. I donned a two-tone bright blue suit, which made me look like Aquaman's bastard cousin! Feeling a new sense of invulnerability, I purchased an underwater camera and went back into the warm, clear water where I spent the next two hours swimming among the breathtaking coral and tropical fish. A most enjoyable time! I returned to land, however, a little dizzy from the relatively rough seas and didn't regain my "land legs" until the following morning.

Our next stop was Canberra (pronounced CAN-bra), capital of Australia. The weather there was a nice departure from our previous days in Ayers Rock and Cairns...72 and mild! Canberra is the only truly planned city in Australia, having been carved out of the bush halfway between Sydney and Melbourne because those two cities could not agree on which would be the capital, and therefore exists for no other reason. In fact, families were deliberately relocated there to colonize the city once it was completed. Canberra is the government seat of Australia and bears a striking resemblance to Washington D.C. The center of town is the Parliament Building and everything radiates out from there in circular streets and other geometric configurations. Even their monuments are erected in perfect lines of sight from each other similar to The Mall in D.C. There's not much to do in Canberra, so our one-day stay was plenty. We toured the botanical gardens, a few government buildings and the War Memorial.

Our final stop was Sydney. My dad and I found ourselves a little lost as to what to do in the cities. Neither of us is what some would call cultured, so the museums and art galleries were not high on our "must-see" list. However, Sydney is a beautiful city with many sites to see. We took a driving tour of the city, complete with a fish-n-chips lunch on the water, but the weather was gray and gloomy, so few photo ops presented themselves. The weather did clear up nicely for us the following day and we took a scenic cruise on the magnificent Sydney harbor aboard a 40ft. sailboat. We also toured the Botanical Gardens, which is known for it's huge population of fruit bats that hang from the trees like ornaments on Dracula's Christmas tree and screech like nails on a chalkboard. Creepy! Our last day, deciding a little culture wouldn't kill us, we toured the famed Sydney Opera House. It's architecture is nothing short of spectacular, and it is one of those unique landmarks that immediately identifies a city. That night we dined on barramundi (tasty fish) and mahi mahi, before indulging in single malt scotch and fine Cuban cigars. I could think of no better way to end a superb trip.

The flight back to the states was just as luxurious, but much less relaxing. Perhaps the realization that we were returning to the real world was disturbing our peace, or perhaps we were excited to see family and friends and share the tales of our journey. For me, I was anxious to return to my new fiancée, who I had been away from for two long weeks. We dined on filet mignon and seafood ragout in the evening while enjoying a movie before drifting off for a restless night's sleep. The omelets, fruit and Starbucks coffee for breakfast helped clear the morning daze as we looked out the window and finally touched down on U.S. soil.

I hope you all enjoyed our adventure as much as my dad and I enjoyed living it!. Cheers, mates!
